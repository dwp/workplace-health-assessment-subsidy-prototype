What is a workplace health assessment

If an employee is finding it difficult to work because of their health, they can get a workplace health assessment.

Workplace health assessments are sometimes called occupational health or vocational rehabilitation assessments.

A workplace health assessment is different from a doctor's report.

It focuses on an employee’s work environment and how their job might affect their health.

A workplace health professional will not diagnose health conditions or disabilities. They will give advice and recommend onward support for an employee's health at work.

The employee may have:

  • a mental health condition, for example anxiety or depression
  • a physical health condition like diabetes or arthritis
  • an injury, such as a broken leg
  • an impairment or physical disability, such as blindness
  • a learning disability, for example Down's syndrome
  • a learning difficultly like dyslexia
  • a neurodivergence like autism or ADHD
  • another condition, disability or illness that might affect their ability to do their job

What happens during a workplace health assessment

An assessment can be remote or in person.

During the assessment, a workplace health professional will ask an employee about:

  • their health and any treatment they're having
  • their usual and expected work activities  
  • their general lifestyle and activities outside work  
  • who is already involved in supporting their health

The conversation is impartial and confidential.

The employee's medical details will not be shared without their consent.

What happens after a workplace health assessment

The workplace health professional will write a report.

The report will contain advice and recommendations, which could include:

  • adjustments to an employee's workspace, such as a bigger screen or adjustable chair
  • a phased return to work or different working hours
  • a referral to other services, like physiotherapy or mental health support  
  • guidance on free and paid-for services employers could use to support an employee
  • help for employers about keeping to health and safety regulations
  • links to other organisations who may be able to help

After the report has been sent, the employer and employee should discuss if any adjustments are needed.

The law on reasonable adjustments

By law, you must make changes if you know, or could be expected to know, that your employee is disabled. These are called reasonable adjustments.

Find out when you must make reasonable adjustments on the Acas (Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service) website (opens in a new tab).

Help with costs from Access to Work

If adjustments are more expensive than is reasonable for you, your employee can apply to Access to Work (opens in new tab) to help with the costs.
